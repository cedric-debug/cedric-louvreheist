Eight Minutes in Paris: The Story of the Louvre Heist

A source-based reconstruction of the October 19, 2025 robbery, how thieves reached the Apollo Gallery, what they took, and what the Louvre changed afterward.

By Cedric  •  December 2025

The Louvre was closed on Sunday, October 19, 2025, after thieves broke into the Apollo Gallery and stole French crown-jewel pieces in a daylight robbery. The break-in turned one of the museum's best-known rooms into the center of a national investigation.

At around 9:30 that morning, the robbery began. In less than eight minutes, a coordinated team carried out a daylight break-in that shut the museum for the day and triggered a national investigation.

According to reporting by the Associated Press, the group used a truck-mounted basket lift to reach a window of the Apollo Gallery, forced their way inside, cut open display cases with power tools, and escaped before police could stop them.

Officials later said the heist lasted less than eight minutes in total, with less than four minutes spent inside the Louvre. By late December 2025, the missing jewels still had not been recovered, even after arrests and new security measures at the window used in the break-in.

How They Got In

The Louvre's own description of the Galerie d'Apollon places it in Room 705 of the Denon Wing, Level 1, and identifies it as the home of the French Crown Jewels. On October 19, the room and the objects inside it proved reachable from outside the building.

Reporting by TIME and The Guardian said the thieves used work gear and a lift to reach the window instead of entering at ground level.

Once inside, they moved directly to the cases. The Guardian later reported that the two men inside spent 3 minutes 58 seconds in the gallery, using disc cutters to open two display cases before descending and fleeing on scooters driven by accomplices.

What Was Stolen

Associated Press reporting listed eight missing objects: a sapphire diadem, necklace and single earring linked to queens Marie-Amélie and Hortense; an emerald necklace and earrings from the matching set of Empress Marie-Louise; a reliquary brooch; and Empress Eugénie's diadem and large corsage-bow brooch.

The Louvre's own gallery description highlights surviving crown-jewel pieces connected to Empress Marie-Louise and Empress Eugénie. Prosecutors later said the estimated value of the missing jewels was 88 million euros, not including their historical importance.

The crown of Empress Eugénie was recovered damaged near the escape route. But by the Louvre's late-December security update, the other missing jewels had still not been found.

The Security Gap

TIME described the Louvre as the world's most-visited museum and said it drew 8.7 million visitors in 2024. After the robbery, attention turned quickly to exterior surveillance and physical barriers as much as to the gallery itself.

On October 31, Associated Press reported that a provisional investigation had found a "chronic, structural underestimation" of theft risk at the museum. The same report cited under-equipped security, ill-suited governance, and obsolete protocols, even though officials said the alarm system itself worked on the day of the robbery.

By December 23, Associated Press reported that workers were installing metal security bars on the window used in the break-in. That follow-up also said all four suspected thieves had been arrested and charged, but the missing jewels still had not been recovered.

Status by December 2025

By the end of December, public reporting had established several facts even as the investigation continued.

  • Arrests All four suspected members of the robbery team had been arrested and charged.
  • Recovery The crown of Empress Eugénie was back in Louvre hands, but the other missing jewels were still unrecovered.
  • Security Metal bars were installed on the window, and French authorities had also promised anti-intrusion and anti-ramming devices on nearby roads.

Other questions, including who commissioned the robbery and where the missing jewels went, remained unresolved in public reporting.

A Nation's Shock

French officials repeatedly framed the robbery as a blow to national heritage. TIME reported that President Emmanuel Macron described it as an attack on French history and heritage.

By October 21, the Associated Press reported that prosecutors valued the missing jewels at 88 million euros and said about 100 investigators were working the case. Later reports tracked arrests, charges, and building upgrades, but the main outcome did not change: at the end of 2025, most of the jewels were still missing.

In the months that followed, French authorities ordered reviews, installed new barriers, and continued the search for the missing jewels. A robbery that lasted only minutes was still shaping security decisions at the Louvre by the end of 2025.
